## Summary
Carnitine palmitoyltransferase (CPT) II deficiency is a disorder of long-chain fatty-acid oxidation. Three clinical phenotypes are typically described: lethal neonatal, severe infantile hepatocardiomuscular, and myopathic. However, a spectrum exists across these phenotypes, leading to a continuum of disease.

## Diagnosis
No consensus clinical diagnostic criteria for carnitine palmitoyltransferase (CPT) II deficiency have been published. CPT II deficiency should be suspected in an infant with an out-of-range newborn screening (NBS) long-chain acylcarnitines result OR in a proband with the following clinical findings (by age), supportive laboratory findings, and family history. Note: A symptomatic individual can have (1) onset of clinical findings prior to availability of NBS results or (2) untreated CPT II deficiency due to NBS not performed or false negative NBS result.

## Clinical Characteristics
Three carnitine palmitoyltransferase (CPT) II deficiency phenotypes are recognized: lethal neonatal, severe infantile hepatocardiomuscular, and myopathic (in which onset ranges from infancy to adulthood). In reality, a spectrum exists across these phenotypes. Onset.

## Genetic Counseling
Carnitine palmitoyltransferase (CPT) II deficiency is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner. Parents of a proband Sibs of a proband Offspring of a proband. The offspring of an individual with CPT II are obligate heterozygotes for a pathogenic variant in CPT2 .
