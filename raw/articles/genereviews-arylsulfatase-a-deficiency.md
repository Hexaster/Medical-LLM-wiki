## Summary
Arylsulfatase A deficiency (also known as metachromatic leukodystrophy or MLD) is characterized by three clinical subtypes: late-infantile, juvenile, and adult MLD. The age of onset within a family is usually similar. The disease course may be from several years in the late-infantile-onset form to decades in the juvenile- and adult-onset forms.

## Diagnosis
Arylsulfatase A deficiency (also known as metachromatic leukodystrophy or MLD) should be suspected in individuals with the following: Arylsulfatase A enzyme pseudodeficiency. Pseudodeficiency is suggested by arylsulfatase A enzyme activity in leukocytes that is 5% to 20% of normal controls. Pseudodeficiency is difficult to distinguish from true arylsulfatase A enzyme deficiency by biochemical testing alone.

## Clinical Characteristics
The clinical presentation of arylsulfatase A deficiency (also known as metachromatic leukodystrophy or MLD) is heterogeneous with respect to the age of onset, the rate of progression, and the initial symptoms. Three clinical subtypes of MLD are primarily distinguished by age of onset: The age of onset within a family is usually similar, but exceptions occur . Although the presenting symptoms and age of onset vary, all individuals eventually develop complete loss of motor, sensory, and cognitive functions.

## Management

Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) is an available therapy that attempts to treat the primary manifestations of MLD in the central nervous system.

## Genetic Counseling
